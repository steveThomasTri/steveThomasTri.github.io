Part 4: Data Analysis

What can we learn from the data that we have grabbed. Coorelations, Standard deviation, and what to do with missing values?

We have our data merged and now what we want to do is see if there are any opportunities to clean our data. The CSV that we have created is (cleaned_NFL_combine_97_to_2018.csv) and this file is what we are going to be reading from now on. We will be using xgboost because it is a package that will handle NaN values, if not, then we will need another package to impute the values. Some of my motivations of using xgboost is this article

A Gentle Introduction to XGBoost for Applied Machine Learning

We have classified our data as supervised, which is that we know what our features are and the data from those features and the target column that will decide whether or not someone is drafted. The jupyter notebook that we did our data analysis is 2 Data Analysis

As you can see, we get a good snapshot of what our data consists of. We have missing values, but because the xgboost library handles these pretty well, we will ignore these for now. The feature with the highest missing values is the 3 cone drill with 26.3%. I have read in some articles that if there are 50% missing values, that column should be dropped. Other articles are in the range of 20-50%, depending on what you are trying to model. Because each event is important for certain positions more so than the next and the fact that he missing values fall short of the 50% threshold, we will keep all of the columns and make a decision on whether to impute missing values or not.

For 2 Data Analysis, I was looking for correlations of two variables. My thought process at the time was that if I can find relationships between two variables, I can best understand my data better. I have got the following strong correlations

  • vertical leap & broad jump (+.804956)
  • weight_lbs & 40 yards (+.858913)
  • 3cone & shuttle (+.790662)
  • broad_jump_in & 40 yards (-.819220)

In notebook 2b Exploratory Data Analysis I have also done an analysis on each of the 6 events to see if there is anything else that I can learn from my data. What I wanted to do is to see if there is a flag for certain data. For example, We will look at the first table where I grouped by position and whether a person is drafted. Wide Receivers need to be fast so it makes sense that we look at how they run the 40 yard dash. People that were drafted had times between 4.21s and 4.76s. People that were not drafted ran between 4.31s and 4.85 seconds. My thought was that any wide receivers that ran less than 4.31 seconds would more likely get drafted and any wide receivers that ran slower than 4.76 seconds were more likely to not be drafted. If the testing data shows that faster than 4.31 seconds were drafted and slower than 4.76 seconds were not drafted, then I believe that there was something there and coaches were looking at the times of the 40 yard, not to say that other factors were considered, but the model might solely look at the 40 yard dash for wide receivers. The notebooks analyze the data by position and whether or not they were drafted is left in the notebook and I am open to what should I have explored further for this data or if this is a fruitless endeavor. We have got to know our data a little bit better, and now we will make our first attempt at modeling our data.
