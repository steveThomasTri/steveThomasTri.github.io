2019 NFL Draft

Taking you through my methodology of whether or not this year's NFL prospects will be drafted.

The one thing that I have learned throughout this process is to be ok with your findings. It was eye opening to witness how much rigor goes into solving a problem.

Steve Thomas 2019

Approximately two months ago, I was and still am fascinated about the world of machine learning. I had looked through tutorials about using the MNIST data set to predict numbers, classes of flowers, whether a picture was a muffin or a puppy. Going through these tutorials I had no problem in trying to set up a neural network or a decision tree or a logistic regression. However, the concept that was not clear to me was if you have set up a model with a certain accuracy, how do you then improve on that accuracy?

Then I went into exploring machine learning packages from python that you can use. Many packages are out there like sci-kit learn, tensorflow, keras, xgboost, etc. I knew that I wanted to do a classification problem, basically answering a Yes or No question, but which one would I want to do? What problem was most interesting to me?

A little background experience of the python technologies that I have used. I used BeautifulSoup, selenium/splinter for automation, flask for backend development. For data visualization, jupyter notebook, pandas, matplotlib. For machine learning, keras, sci-kit learn, tensorflow. It was time to put these to use and see if I can find a problem and attempt to solve. In our class, there was a lot of football talk about the Cleveland Browns this year and how they could be a playoff team. So I was thinking, how team select players and what is the process that prospects go through. There are "analysts" and "experts" that predict where would players go, when would they go off the board in a draft, what round are they picked. Although these questions are great for an advanced model, I just want to predict whether a player would get drafted. And so, the data science problem was stated.

Based on an NFL draft prospects height, weight, and the results that they posted in the NFL combine, will this person be drafted?

As you read through this article, pay special attention to green boxes, because over the course of two months, the main question changes as I learn new methods and ways to represent the data. Now that I have the question at hand, how would we retrieve the data?

The process of the NFL draft

Before we get into the data acquisition, A word on how the draft works. In late April, for three days, Americans are glued to watch which players will be drafted to which teams. Usually day 1 has the most talented prospects and that generates the most buzz. Day 2 consists of rounds 2 and 3. The conclusion of the draft is day 3 for rounds 4 to 7. There are 32 teams in the NFL and each team gets a pick for each round. A total of 224 people are drafted plus 32 additional picks for a total of 256 players that are drafted. These picks are compulsory unless they trade. For further reading on how the draft works with the twists and turns, read the NFL's article titled The Rules of the draft

But what is a draft without prospects. That is where coaches get a look at how athletic each prospect is. How well they interview with coaches. How well they test and other factors. Two months earlier, 338 prospects were invited to Indianapolis. The link of all of the invitees is here.

Most of the prospects will compete in at least one of 6 testing events in the combine:

  1. Bench Press - The weights are set at 225 lbs. and the person has to do as many reps as possible. This is an indicator of their strength.
  2. Vertical Leap - The prospect starts standing, they will jump as high as they can to slap a wall. This is an indicator of their lower body strength and power
  3. Broad Jump - The prospect starts flat footed and then they leap out in front. This is an indicator of their lower body strength and power.
  4. Three Cone Relay - The course is an L shaped. They will start at a cone, run to the middle cone, run back to the start, then run around both cones before sprinting to the start. This is an indicator of how a prospect can quickly shift directions at high speed.
  5. 20 yard Shuttle Run - A prospect starts at a line, sprints 5 yards forward, sprints 10 yards back, and then sprints 5 yards forward to complete the run. This is an indicator of the prospects ability to change direction and his lateral quickness.
  6. 40 yard dash - A prospect sprints down 40 yards as quick as possible. This is an indication of their explosiveness, speed, burst, and acceleration.

This is the data that we will be testing once all of the models are complete. We have our test data, but now we need data to train through data acquisition.

So in this case, we will now revise our main question.

Having at least participated in one NFL combine event, will a future prospect be drafted in the NFL?

The drafthistory.com website provides us with the years of every NFL player that has been drafted. All that we are looking for is if the player is on the list, then we consider them drafted. Time to Think Outside the Pocket

Although in our first model we were able to get 70.21% accuracy, the problem is that this model doesn't accurately represent what the coaches are looking for. Because NFL teams need to fill different positions and different positions require different attributes, the first model is not a fair representation of what is happening in the real world. It is not enough to think about the measurements of a player, you also have to think about the intangibles of that individual. You have to put on many hats as a data scientist, such as in this case, a coach, a scout, a GM, an owner, etc. How do you measure leadership? How do you evaluate how well they interview? What is their work ethic on and off the field? More importantly, how would one quantify intangibles? For some of these intangibles, they are not available to the public and thus it may be impossible to measure like interviews, white board test, Wonderlic test. One word about the Wonderlic test is that some of these scores are leaked to the public and we cannot verify the validity of these scores. Here is a list of factors that were considered and not considered. Keep in mind that the items not considered may not be an exhaustive list.

Factors that I have considered:
  • Prospect's height and weight
  • Prospect's measurements in the 6 events
  • Prospect's college stats
  • If any awards were received (Heisman, all-American)
  • Prospect's position
Factors that I have NOT considered:
  • Prospect's arm length and hand length
  • Prospect's college and conference
  • Prospect's interviews, white board test, and Wonderlic test
  • How tough was their conference
  • Their grit (leadership, work ethic, toughness, football intelligence)
  • Different teams needs to fill certain positions
  • Any tapes that scouts looked at to assess their playing ability
  • And much more...

There are other factors that were not considered and I have a list of two articles that outline these intangibles. The first article is from The Ringer one year ago. This article outlines how scouts evaluate qualities like poise, confidence, grit, etc. The second article is a starting point on how the Bleacher Report breaks down certain positions. The article starts off with what scouts look for in a safety. There are more links in other positions if you are interested. There are other articles out there, but these are the two main ones to get a general understanding on other factors than just raw numbers.

From suggestions that were given to me. I have decided that instead of one model, I would have a model for each group. With each model, determine what combine events are important in an attempt to reduce noise and make the models more of like what to look for in those positions. So starting from our 8 original features, let's see if there are some features that we would like to add, or delete.

Feature Selection/Feature Engineering

Feature Engineering is taking the data that you have already to generate new features. Sometimes it is an attempt to explain features not considered in the model. For example, the feature that we want to add is BMI (Body Mass Index) we can use the prospect's height and weight to generate what their BMI is so that we may be able to predict if someone is drafted. You can see that you can generate any number of additional features based on the data you have, but the risk is that you are going to introduce unnecessary noise to your model. Feature engineering is an art and a science, so the only way to know what constitutes a good feature is by experience.

What we will do is to categorize the data that we have into ten groups with ten separate csvs. These ten categories are:

  • Offensive Line (OL) consists of OT, OG, C, OL
  • Defensive Line (DL) consists of DT, DE, DL
  • Running Backs (RB) consists of RB, HB, FB
  • Line Backers (LB) consists of OLB, ILB, LB, DB
  • Tight Ends (TE)
  • Cornerbacks (CB)
  • Wide Receivers (WR)
  • Safety (S) consists of FS, SS
  • Quarterbacks (QB)
  • Punters (P)

So let's revise our question:

Based on an NFL draft prospects height, weight, and the results that they posted in the NFL combine, will this person be drafted if we group them by position?

So we get away from a position neutral model to a model based on a players declared position in the combine. We start off separating each player in the training data by the categories. So we will breakdown the two tables above. Starting from the left, Using the original 8 features, we get the base accuracy and a possible accuracy if any of the 8 features are removed. The next line is any features that we will add. For different groups, there will be certain featurs that make sense to add like wide receivers receiving yards, touchdowns, that do not for a defense tackle. After we add more features, we run the model again and this time we get the highest accuracy attainable if we do not do any feature tuning and any features that we will remove.

The second table is once we get the maximum accuracy, we will be doing hyperparameter tuning to see if we can get the model to perform better. There are 6 parameters that we have considered tuning:

  • Learning Rate - In each step, as new data is taken in, the model will change how certain features are weighed in each step. The larger the value, the more rows it learns quickly.
  • n_estimators - Number of decision trees
  • Max Depth - How deep is each tree
  • Minimun Child Weight - A larger value makes the model more conservative
  • Gamma - Minimum loss reduction required to make further partitions on a leaf node of the tree.
  • Colsample_bytree - subsample ratio of columns when constructing each tree.

For more information on each of these parameters, consult the documentation. XGBoost Parameters

Here is a breakdown of what each notebook was used for:

  • The first notebook establishes the model from the base configurations and finds the maximum accuracy if the original 8 features are considered.
  • Part II Notebook uses splinter to obtain additional data based on the group. It goes through each name and fills in the data and if they got awards. College stats are left blank if none exists, but awards are always populated.
  • Part III notebook gets the base accuracy when we add features from part II. Then we find the maximum accuracy if certain features are removed. Verify that removing those features give us the maximum accuracy. Then we implement hyperparameter tuning using the 6 parameters through brute force. There are 14,400 different combinations to check for.
